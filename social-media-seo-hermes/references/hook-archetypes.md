# Slide 1 Hook Archetypes

Slide 1 is **just the hook**. It earns the swipe — nothing else. It does not have to share a setting with slides 2–6, doesn't have to feature the dish, doesn't even have to feature the venue. It can be anything that stops the thumb.

The **text** on slide 1 always comes from [hooks.md](hooks.md) — pick a hook line first (by category, restaurant fit, memory, trends), then pick the visual archetype below that supports it.

This file is about **the visual** for slide 1. Five archetypes, pick whichever fits the hook line + scenario best.

---

## Archetype 1: Object Hook

A close-up of a non-food object that implies the night.

**When to use**: when the hook line is mood-setting (*"Friday nights are for this"*, *"Date night plan ✓"*) or when the dish itself is on slide 3, not slide 1.

**Examples**:
- Car keys + lipstick on a bathroom counter, soft warm light
- A phone screen showing a reservation confirmation, hand barely in frame
- Rain on a restaurant window, blurred yellow lights inside
- Coats hanging on a wooden hook, restaurant interior soft-focus behind
- Two empty wine glasses on a marble bar, shadow of a hand reaching in

**Prompt language**: *"Tight crop, soft focus background, single hero object, warm low-key lighting, smartphone candid feel."*

---

## Archetype 2: Character Pre-Context

The character we'll see in slides 2–6, but earlier — before they arrive at the restaurant.

**When to use**: when the hook line is personal/aspirational (*"Treat yourself"*, *"Date night ready"*) and the scenario has clear character continuity (e.g. `date-night`, `friend-night-dinner`).

**Examples** (calibrate to scenario's `characters`):
- A woman applying lipstick in a mirror, golden bathroom light
- Two friends getting ready, one zipping up a jacket, one checking her phone
- A couple stepping out of a taxi, restaurant facade behind them softly lit
- Someone walking down a street toward warm-lit restaurant windows, back of head

**Honest tradeoff**: the face/outfit on slide 1 won't *exactly* match slide 2 (slide 2 is the character seed for slides 3–6). For the hook archetype to land, the **archetype** of person needs to match — same age range, same outfit register, same energy. The viewer doesn't compare faces frame-by-frame; they swipe.

**Prompt language**: *"Pre-restaurant moment. Same age and style as the dining-room scene that follows. Faces partial, blurred, or over-shoulder OK. Casual smartphone candid composition."*

---

## Archetype 3: Place Hook

The exterior or facade of the restaurant, dramatically composed.

**When to use**: when the hook line is location-anchored (*"Tucked away in [neighborhood]"*, *"This is the [city] spot"*) or when the venue itself is visually distinctive (great signage, beautiful entry, iconic block).

**Examples**:
- Restaurant exterior at dusk, sign lit, no people, painterly composition
- View through the front window from the street, blurred figures inside, warm tones
- Restaurant facade at night, rain-slick pavement, neon glow

**Reference**: pass an exterior photo from the venue Drive folder (if it exists) as a reference.

**Prompt language**: *"Restaurant exterior, [time-of-day], cinematic composition, warmth from inside, smartphone-candid feel."*

---

## Archetype 4: Detail Hook

A very tight crop of something that hints at what's coming — almost abstract.

**When to use**: when the hook line is curiosity-driven (*"Wait. They make this from scratch??"*, *"This is the [dish] [city] was missing"*) and the dish or its prep is the implicit subject. The detail is intriguing precisely because it's *not* the obvious money shot.

**Examples**:
- Hand twirling pasta on a fork in low light, almost abstract, motion blur
- Wine being poured, dramatic motion blur, dark background
- A cracked egg yolk over pasta, extreme close-up, no plate visible
- Steam rising from a pan, hand barely in frame
- A single garnish (basil leaf, pepper grind) mid-fall onto a dish

**Prompt language**: *"Extreme close-up. Tight composition. Hint at the dish without showing the full plate. Motion or steam preferred. Cinematic, low-key."*

**Note**: this can use a dish photo as a loose visual anchor (color/texture/lighting), but the composition is the script's job — don't replicate the dish photo's framing.

---

## Archetype 5: Text-Led

The image is secondary; the overlay text does the work.

**When to use**: when the hook line is short, declarative, and self-sufficient (*"Friday night ✓"*, *"For [price] you get this"*, *"6 hours of prep. 12 minutes to eat."*) — number-driven or punchy formats work especially well.

**Examples**:
- A clean abstract photo (wine glass against dark, single candle flame, blurred neon, hand on a tabletop) with the hook text dominating
- A phone-shot moody color field (deep red velvet, dark wood grain, candlelit white tablecloth)
- A tightly-cropped texture (cheese rind, charred crust, ice-cube reflection) as a backdrop

**Prompt language**: *"Moody backdrop image, single texture or surface, low contrast, leaves the top two-thirds open for overlay text. Cinematic, warm."*

**Pairing**: works best with the **Number-in-the-hook** modifier from `hooks.md` (*"6 hours of prep. 12 minutes to eat."*) and the **Reaction / Disbelief** category (*"Wait. They make this from scratch??"*).

## Picking the Archetype

| Hook category (from hooks.md) | Strongest archetypes |
|---|---|
| Story-Behind-the-Dish | Detail Hook, Object Hook |
| Reaction / Disbelief | Detail Hook, Text-Led |
| Price Reveal | Text-Led, Object Hook |
| Sourcing / Provenance | Detail Hook, Place Hook |
| Behind-the-Scenes / Chef POV | Detail Hook, Character Pre-Context (chef getting ready) |
| Reservation Urgency | Place Hook, Object Hook (calendar / clock / phone) |
| Comparison / Contrast | Detail Hook, Text-Led |
| Question Hook | Text-Led, Character Pre-Context |
| Controversial / Contrarian | Text-Led, Object Hook |
| Seasonal / Calendar | Detail Hook (the seasonal ingredient close-up), Place Hook |
| Collaboration / Event | Place Hook, Character Pre-Context |
| Review Highlight / Social Proof | Text-Led, Place Hook |

Default when uncertain: **Detail Hook** — the most visually arresting and the easiest to generate well. Reserve **Character Pre-Context** for posts where the social moment is the headline (date night, friend night, anniversary).

---

## Faces on Slide 1

Same rule as the rest of the carousel: **faces can be partial, blurred, or out-of-frame.** Slide 1's job is the swipe, not the face.

If the archetype is Character Pre-Context and you want a face visible, prefer:
- Side profile (lipstick mirror, phone scrolling)
- Three-quarter back of head (walking toward, sitting down)
- Soft focus / motion blur (in-the-middle-of-doing-something)

Avoid front-facing portraits with sharp facial features unless the upstream image gen has been confirmed to handle that scenario well for this restaurant. Otherwise expect uncanny-valley results.
